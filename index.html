Why Public Policy for Open Data?

  • Understand

  • The first step to the “How” of open data policy is understanding the “Why” of open data policy. So, come to think of it, why policy? Why not simply publish data?

    Public policy is crucial because it shows public commitment to lasting change. In contrast to simply releasing datasets ad hoc, establishing a strong policy framework lays the groundwork for public confidence in continuous access to city data; It compels participation across all city departments, not simply those that choose voluntarily to share data; and policy invites the community into the process, ultimately ensuring public oversight of the long-term availability of public information--even politically charged information--in ways that purely administrative release of data cannot.

    In short, truly public data demands the oversight and involvement of the public and this can only be solidified through public policy.

Leveraging Existing Context

  • Build on What Exists

  • Successful open data policy does not exist in a vacuum, but rather builds upon existing processes and structures within city hall, both formal and informal. It is therefore crucial for open data advocates to develop a strategy and approach to public policy development that leverages a city's existing legal, political, and administrative context to the full extent possible.

    What are existing practices in internal data management? What is the current process for responding to public records requests? How might open data fit within or advance existing city goals or mission statement such as a comprehensive community plan, or citywide engagement strategy?

    Only with this important context in mind can a city hall make informed decisions about strategy--like whether to pursue an update to existing policy, the development of entirely new policy, or both--and about program design--like who should oversee open data implementation, and will they need more staff to do it?--in ways that leverage existing context to the advantage of open data, rather than to its disadvantage.

Setting Policy Objectives for Broad Appeal

  • Different cities have different reasons for pursuing open data policy, as do different community groups and individuals. What are your city’s open data policy objectives and who cares enough about those objectives to help?

  • Define Open Data Policy Objectives

  • Whether it's about advancing transparency and accountability, inviting community engagement and collaboration, or about fostering civic-technology and informed decision making, there are no shortage of reasons for pursuing open data policy. Whatever a city's reasons, it is important for those developing open data policy to clarify the intended outcomes and aspiration with an explicit statement of goals, values or purpose.

  • Explore Best Practices for Open Data Policies

  • Join cities across the U.S. that are using open data and evidence to improve the lives of residents.

  • Build a Coalition

  • Setting and circulating expectations for open data policy will not only help primary advocates, but will also play an important role in the formation and growth of of a true open data team. Stated objectives will invite logical allies and stakeholders from within government and from the community. It will be worth listening to their feedback and spending the up-front time to understand the full spectrum of open data’s organizational value to city hall as well as its community value to those outside of government. This time and effort will pay dividends by helping to surface specific issues and even specific datasets that might not otherwise have been considered, and which can be critical to creating the buy-in needed for enacting meaningful policy.

  • Memorialize a Shared Vision

  • A working statement of policy goals can and should be revised and iterated as additional feedback is received throughout the process of crafting open data policy. And ultimately the refined objectives, now building upon the shared values of the broader community should be represented in the policy itself, something often done in the form of “Whereas” clauses.

Engaging Stakeholders in the Policy Process

  • Craft Policy in the Open

  • Throughout the entire open data process it’s important to align with all relevant stakeholders on both the open data policy and program objectives and the strategy being pursued. This means both those inside and those outside of city hall, and it also means any and everyone who might have an interest or who might be impacted by new open data policy.

  • This engagement is especially critical as the policy language itself is being drafted.

  • Hosting internal and public meetings is one tried-and-true approach. However, many cities are meeting the public where they are, online, utilizing collaborative online approaches to policy making to empower external stakeholders to participate in the open data process in a substantive way. Build Support, Enact, and Implement

  • It’s not enough to simply draft a policy, it needs to be formally adopted and implemented to move the city toward its identified open data objectives.

  • Building support for policy will be made easier by defining policy objectives with broad appeal and responding to feedback throughout the open data policy development process. However, those who need to approve the policy (executives, politicians, legal advisors, etc.) may give bills or other policy drafts more critical scrutiny once a draft (and the changes it hopes to bring about) is officially on the docket for consideration. If the public or other stakeholders have been involved, they can serve as an ally, giving testimony at public hearings and otherwise showing support.

  • What’s more, a robust open data policy will create a clear picture of what implementation means including required actions and timelines for those responsible. The public should be kept in the know and departments may need to be supported and updated as they make the changes necessary to comply with new processes. City halls should be sure to monitor progress toward successful implementation through regular review of the open data policy and program.

Announcing Your Policy

  • Promote

  • A successful open data program requires sustained public participation. You can begin engaging the public by building awareness of your city’s open data program. . This means announcing and promoting a new or updated open data policy framework. And for the public to know if the policy is working, you should should also share your city’s progress toward making data more accessible to the public by including newly released datasets, projects created using the city’s data and other key successes.
